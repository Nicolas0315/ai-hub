# Radiohead - OK Computer - Music Review
# Source: Style of Pitchfork/NME criticism

OK Computer is the sound of a band staring into the digital abyss and somehow making it beautiful. Radiohead's third album abandons the anthemic guitar rock of The Bends for something far more unsettling and ambitious — a sprawling, paranoid masterpiece that predicted our tech-addled future with eerie precision.

From the opening crash of "Airbag" to the glacial fade of "The Tourist," the album maintains a constant tension between warmth and alienation. Thom Yorke's voice floats over landscapes of shimmering guitars and electronic textures like a ghost haunting a machine. There's genuine terror here — the frantic breakdown of "Paranoid Android," the crushing weight of "Climbing Up the Walls" — but also moments of devastating tenderness, like the aching beauty of "Let Down" or the resigned grace of "No Surprises."

What makes OK Computer endure isn't just its prescient themes of technological anxiety and consumer emptiness, but the way it sounds. Every track feels simultaneously intimate and vast, as if the band found a way to make loneliness echo across cathedrals. It's an album that makes you feel the cold glass of a screen pressing against your face while somehow also offering the comfort of knowing someone else feels it too.

A landmark record that redefined what rock music could be.
