Sonata form is a large-scale musical structure consisting of three main sections: exposition, development, and recapitulation. In the exposition, the primary theme (P) is stated in the tonic key, followed by a transition (TR) that modulates to a secondary key. The secondary theme (S) appears in the new key — typically the dominant (V) in major-key works or the mediant (III) in minor-key works — and concludes with the essential expositional cadence (EEC). The exposition is usually repeated. The development section explores harmonic instability through modulations, sequences (typically 4-8 measure models forming "cores"), and thematic fragmentation. No stable key area is established. The recapitulation restates the primary theme in the tonic, adjusts the transition to remain in tonic, and transposes the secondary theme to the tonic key, thereby resolving the tonal conflict established in the exposition. A coda may follow. The form derives from harmonically open, rounded binary form and is characterized by tonal conflict (tonic vs. secondary key) and its eventual resolution. Repeat signs typically mark the exposition and then development plus recapitulation together.
