# Target: Modal Jazz - General Audience Explanation
# URL: https://blog.oup.com/2014/08/miles-davis-kind-of-blue-classic-jazz-album/

Imagine jazz musicians in the 1950s as race car drivers navigating a twisting course at breakneck speed — every few seconds, the road turns, and they have to react instantly. That was bebop: brilliant, dizzying, technically astonishing, but exhausting to play and sometimes to listen to.

Modal jazz was Miles Davis saying: what if we straightened the road? What if, instead of navigating through dozens of chord changes, we gave the soloist one scale and let them explore it — deeply, freely, with all the time in the world?

The result was a revolution in how jazz sounded and felt. Instead of the nervous, angular energy of bebop, modal jazz created vast open spaces — sonic landscapes where a single note could hang in the air and mean something. The rhythm section stopped "going places" harmonically and instead created a steady, hypnotic foundation, like a still pond that the soloist could send ripples across.

This wasn't simplification — it was a different kind of complexity. Without chord changes to provide structure, the improviser had to find direction from melody alone, relying on storytelling instinct rather than technical navigation. The best modal jazz performances have a quality of meditation about them — each phrase seems to arise from deep listening, from the spaces between notes as much as the notes themselves.

What Miles Davis discovered was that sometimes less really is more — that harmonic simplicity could create emotional depth that no amount of virtuosic chord-running could match.
