# Source: Monet Water Lilies (1906) - Formal/Technical Analysis
# URL: https://www.artic.edu/articles/862/color-chemistry-and-creativity-in-monets-water-lilies

Monet's Water Lilies (1906, Art Institute of Chicago) employs a vibrant palette of synthetic pigments with layered impasto technique. Colors: French ultramarine (warm reddish-blue) and cobalt blue (cooler) mixed for infinite blue tones on the water surface, with lead white for opacity and texture. Red lakes with vermilion in swirls for flowers. Viridian (alone or mixed with synthetic malachite and yellows) for sunlit leaves. Cobalt violet (cobalt arsenate) for purple shadows and accents on lilies. Composition: canvas fills edge-to-edge with water and lilies, omitting the horizon — a radical break from Renaissance traditions — positioning viewers as if floating above, with no scale anchor. Technique: wet-in-wet and dry paint layered for textural depth; thick impasto with short gestural dashes, vertical strokes, and dabs of broken color (pale yellows/greens, red/lilac spots, vague blues for shadows) that vibrate optically without blending. Pigment trapped in canvas weave creates sculptural effect. Multiple painting sessions build underlying colors visible through open brush networks. Later works show greenish-blue dominance, possibly due to cataracts filtering yellow vision.
