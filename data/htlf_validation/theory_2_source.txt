A fugue is a contrapuntal compositional technique in two or more voices, characterized by the systematic imitation of a principal theme called the subject. The subject is introduced successively in each voice at the beginning (exposition) and developed throughout the piece. Key structural elements: (1) Subject — the main melodic theme, stated first by one voice. (2) Answer — imitation of the subject at the interval of a perfect fifth (real answer) or with modifications to preserve tonal coherence (tonal answer). (3) Countersubject — a secondary melodic line accompanying the subject/answer, often in invertible (double) counterpoint so voices can exchange positions. (4) Exposition — opening section where all voices enter successively with subject/answer, establishing tonic and dominant keys; a bridge (codetta) may modulate between entries. (5) Episodes — contrasting sections between subject entries, developing material through sequences, inversions, or augmentations. Advanced techniques include stretto (overlapping subject entries before one is complete), augmentation (lengthening note values), diminution (shortening), inversion (reversing interval directions), and retrograde (playing the subject backwards).
