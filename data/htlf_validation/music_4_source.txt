Stravinsky's The Rite of Spring (1913) employs irregular rhythmic accents, polymetric effects, bitonal/polytonal harmony, and layered orchestration. "Augurs of Spring" uses pounding chords with off-beat sforzando accents in the French horns; the opening chord blends an F-flat major triad with an E-flat dominant seventh, creating extreme dissonance. Core harmonic structures build on perfect fifths (filled as major/minor triads), often pairing two such chords a semitone apart. The piece mixes diatonic, octatonic, and chromatic pitch collections with no tonic or cadences providing harmonic direction. Polytonality layers clashing keys (e.g., horns' quadratonic melody over building textures). Orchestration builds density incrementally: strings start with quick repeated figures, clarinets and violins add swoops, horns return motifs, building to full tutti. Percussion and brass dominate military/religious scenes. Short melodic fragments based on perfect fourth/fifth frames repeat as ostinatos. Folk influences include Lithuanian tunes. The rhythmic irregularity prevents predictable patterns — strings and winds alternate accented dissonant chords with unpredictable descending/repeated motifs.
