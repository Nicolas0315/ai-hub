Mark Rothko's No. 61 (Rust and Blue) (1953), oil on canvas, 292.74 cm × 233.68 cm, Museum of Contemporary Art, Los Angeles. A Color Field painting featuring large, hazy rectangular blocks of layered color: glossy burgundy/rust rising from the bottom, an unbroken blue strip in the center, and foggy gray-purple at the top. Dark borders frame all edges. Composition: canvas divides into soft-edged horizontal color fields without representational forms. Uneven, hazy borders suggest containment and infinity. Technique: thin washes of oil (sometimes mixed with egg-based media) creating luminous "inner light" effect. Paint visibly flows upward due to Rothko's technique of inverting the canvas during creation. Subtle drips and upward flows reveal process. Three translucent layers build depth through layering, producing a glowing, immersive quality that shifts with light and viewing distance. Scale: monumental at over 2.9 meters tall, designed to envelop the viewer. No representational forms, no horizon line, no figures.
