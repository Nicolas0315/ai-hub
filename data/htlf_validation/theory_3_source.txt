Modal jazz is a style of jazz that uses musical modes (scales) rather than chord progressions as the harmonic framework for improvisation. Instead of navigating through rapidly changing chord sequences (as in bebop), soloists improvise over static or slowly changing modal centers. Common modes employed include Dorian (minor mode built on the second degree of the major scale), Mixolydian (built on the fifth degree), Phrygian (built on the third degree, with characteristic flat second), and Aeolian (natural minor). Typical modal jazz compositions use one or two chords sustained over many measures (vamp), allowing the soloist extended time to explore melodic possibilities within a single scale. The rhythm section maintains the modal center through repeated bass patterns and sustained chord voicings rather than progressing through functional harmony (ii-V-I patterns). This approach prioritizes horizontal (melodic) thinking over vertical (harmonic) thinking. Key practitioners: Miles Davis, John Coltrane, McCoy Tyner, Herbie Hancock. Emerged in the late 1950s as a reaction against the harmonic complexity of bebop.
